Mary Shelley

Early Life & Background

Mary Shelley was born in 1797 in London, named Mary Wollstonecraft Godwin. Her life began in a very unusual and chaotic way. Her mother had written about women's rights, and her father had been a philosopher and writer. This meant that Mary had been born into a world of large thoughts and strict ideas.

Mary's mother had died just days after giving birth to her. This had a large crater on Mary's life, she had never actually met her mother. Mary had grown up understanding that her mother had been a very important woman. Mary had felt that she had to live up to her mother, to make her proud.

Mary's father had brought her up and had ensured that she had been well-educated. This was great! Mary had been encouraged to read a lot and had been encouraged to think deeply, booting up her literature career. Mary's life had not been easy. Mary's father had remarried, and Mary had not gotten along at all with her stepmother.

She also spent time near her mother’s grave, which can reflect on how much loss shaped her early life. These experiences later influenced the dark and emotional themes in her writing.

Relationship with Percy Shelley

When she was a teenager, she met Percy Bysshe Shelley, who was a famous poet and also shared her father's big ideological views. They fell in love, even though he was already married at that time. They ran away together in 1814, which caused a big scene. This event was disapproved of by the people, and Mary felt somewhat isolated.

Their relationship was rocky. They encountered financial troubles and personal loss. Mary’s children died at birth, which caused Mary a lot of grief and suffering. These are all significant factors that influenced Mary’s successful views on life and death. Mary’s life was very troublesome

Creation of Frankenstein

Mary Shelley is certainly best known for her novel "Frankenstein," which she started working on when she was only 18 years old. Mary Shelley got her inspiration to write this book when she went to Switzerland with her husband, Percy, and others, including Lord Byron. They decided to challenge one another to come up with the scariest ghost stories. Mary Shelley was having a hard time thinking of a ghost story, but she got an idea that would become the inspiration for "Frankenstein." "Frankenstein" is a story about a scientist named Victor Frankenstein who creates life and rejects his creation. This creation was lonely and unloved and became angry and violent.

This novel is quite different from previous Gothic novels. Instead of exploring just fear and mystery, "Frankenstein" by Mary Shelley goes deeper into the following themes:

  • What happens when people try to disturb and control nature
  • What happens when people have too much ambition
  • What happens when people need love and acceptance

This novel is also related to some of the first premature thoughts about science and electricity, which were just being developed and recognised at this time.

Writing Style and Ideas

Mary Shelley’s writing focuses more on the biggest unsolved questions than just creating basic fear. Her stories often deal with:

  • Life and death
  • Responsibility
  • Loneliness
  • The effects of rejection

    • She doesn't explain everything in simple terms. In Frankenstein, horror in the story is real as if the creature exists in the gothic world. This makes the horror in the story more frightening because it cannot be directly explained.

    Another really successful technique she uses in her writings is strong emotions. The creature in "Frankenstein" is not only a monster but also a victim. It has standard human feelings such as pain, rejection, and anger. This creates a sense of thought in the minds of readers about who is actually responsible? This is one reason her writings have stood out as very unique and creative.

    Later Life Struggles and Death

    Mary’s life continued to be rough and difficult. In 1822, Percy Bysshe Shelley passed away after a boating accident. This left Mary all alone with her only son. Mary worked hard to earn a living after the death of Percy, the emotional breakdown had affected the rest of her life.

    She continued to write novels like "Valperga" and "The Last Man," but none of them attained the fame that "Frankenstein" had achieved. She worked very hard to secure her husband's literature legacy by publishing his poems.

    Mary Shelley sadly died in 1851 in London. Like Ann Radcliffe, her life ended without major public drama, but her influence continued to grow after her death.

    Conclusion

    Mary Shelley had a big impact on literature and Gothic history. "Frankenstein" could be considered one of the first works of science fiction, as it has a scientific creation concept. "Frankenstein" could also be considered one of the first Gothic novels in its history.

    Her work influenced later writers and thinkers, including Edgar Allan Poe. The story of Frankenstein has also become part of the popular culture, with the creature appearing in films, plays, and other media.

    What makes her particularly significant is that she was able to incorporate in elements of storytelling with philosophical issues. She encouraged her readers to think about humanity, science, and the importance of having a kind behaviour, while still actually entertaining the reader.
